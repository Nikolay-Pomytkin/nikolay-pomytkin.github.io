    This lecture's topics of privacy, information security, and algorithmic bias. Since I am a computer science major, these topics are pretty important and significant in my career.     Online Week 2 (4/6-4/10):

    The framework that we reviewed this week was the concept of "whistleblowers". A whistleblower is someone (usually an employee or member of an organization) that exposes a wrongdoing by a company or organization to the government/relevant law enforcement agencies. "Whistleblowers play a hugely important role in countering the worst corporate and government excesses. "- Damon Poeter Protecting whistleblowers is a crucial step to ensuring that companies, organizations, and even government agencies don't make unethical decisions. Something that was covered in this lecture was engineers and their role in whistleblowing. Before we left campus, we watched a video about Edward Snowden, an analyst that worked in the NSA for many years, and how he came out with information about how the NSA was spying on regular people and abusing their intelligence sources. Edward Snowden is just once example of how engineers can "blow the whistle" on wrongdoing at the companies they work for. In the powerpoint, we reviewed how engineers are often pressured by the organizations they work for which means they are reluctant to become a whistleblower when an ethical dilemma actually does arise. I think something especially important to think about is how much responsibility engineers should take on in terms of speaking up against negative behavior by their companies. Personally, I believe that company employees should try to work out the problem from within the organization first, but if their complaints are not acted upon, engineers have an ethical responsibility to blow the whistle and expose these issues publically. People have been hurt and have even died in accidents that have resulted from unethical practices by companies where engineers failed to speak up. Although engineers should always work to innovate and make the world a better place through problem solving, engineers are also responsible for ensuring the safety of the users of their products.

    Interview

    Interview Reflection:

    Both the listening ladder and the levels of listening concepts are great tools for self-reflecting on our communication habits. We talk to other people every single day, about ourselves and whats going on in our lives, about the news and other random topics, but we rarely take a moment to reflect on how we really listen to what others are saying. When we first went over the listening ladder, something that I noticed in particular was how different levels of the ladder put different focus on how much the listener was focusing on understanding the listener and the meaning of the message they're trying to put across. We can see a similar theme with the "levels of listening". Reflecting on my interview with my friend Teo, something I noticed was that even though I was aware of the fact that I should be trying to be an active listener and try to understand what my interviewee was explaining, I kept on spending time thinking about the next question I was going to ask, before he even finished talking. Although I was trying to "become curious about the other and open to new perspectives" as listening level 2 describes, I wasn't full engaged in just trying to appreciate what he was saying. I definitely felt comfortable talking to him and asking personal questions, nut I felt like I wasn't completely involved as I was intermittently taking notes, and not putting my full focus onto listening and understanding the stories he was telling. Listening is a skill that can be developed just like any other skill, and I think the next step for me to improve that skill is working to be a more active and empathetic listener. Rather than just trying to absorb the information I'm hearing, I need to work to understand what is being said and be more aware of the context and emotional meaning of the conversation. Not only did this interview activity help me reflect more on my conversational and listening skills, but it also allowed me to get a deeper understanding about what motivates one of my closest friends.

    The person I chose to interview for this assignment was one of my best-friends, Teo Nys. Teo and I have been friends since elementary school, and we've spent a lot of time getting to know each other really well over the years. I tried to set up the interview from the start as if I didn't know him, asking more general questions that someone would probably get from a recruiter at a job interview, but then I led into slightly more personal questions by diving deeper on some of the main things Teo revealed about himself while introducing himself. One of the themes that was recurring throughout our conversation was the idea of culture and identity: how much of an impact culture has on our identity. Teo brought up his German and Russian ancestry (Russian father and a German mother), and how he thinks that some of his personal values are closely related to his european origins. Teo explained how he prioritizes quality over quantity in his personal relationships; he believes that his upbringing has resulted in him valuing building stronger ties with his close circle of friends. To quote Teo directly: "I don't seek that constant attention and validation from others. I like to spend time with my close friends." Another topic that came up was the idea of "soft" and "hard/technical" skills.

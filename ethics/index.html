Lecture Reflections

This section includes reflections on concepts covered in each lecture (one reflection per lecture). The reflections may include references to what occured during class time on that particular day, but is mostly meant to be a reflection of how I felt about the material that we were going over at the time. I have written one paragraph per class period (and 2 paragraphs per week for the second half of the semester).

Lecture 1 (2/4/20):

This was actually the first lecture of ENEE200 that I attended, since I joined the class late off of the waitlist. For me, this lecture was a great intro to the course. I remember what happened during this lecture pretty clearly, even when writing about it weeks later. I remember it being a bit awkward at first, because I wasn't sure what lecture group I was in. For the first few minutes of lecture I aimlessly walked around trying to figure out what group I was supposed to be with, until finally approaching Dr. Mogul and getting placed with the group that I ended up working with for the first quarter of the semester. We were introduced to the first ethical framework of the semester: ABET and "the Good Engineer". This was my first introduction to the idea of ethical frameworks in this class, and it was genuinely the first example (of many throughout the course) of concepts that change the way I thought about engineering.

Looking back at my notes from that day, something that stands out to me is the list of social factors that are changing modern engineering:

  • independent contractor engineers vs. working at large firms
  • managerial engineer vs. practicing engineer
  • increased complexity of products requires increased funding of research
  • (the above factor results in) rising financial stakes

    • I had never really thought about engineering in this way, and it was definitely quite eye-opening. I left this lecture with a furthered interest in engineering ethics and looking forward to coming back on thursday.

    Lecture 2 (2/11/20):

    During this second lecture, we started diving deeper into concrete ethics concepts, more specifically the definition of ethics and how codes of conducts reflect ethical values of different groups of people and organizations. This lecture was particularly memorable because it was when my first lecture group started to get to know each other well (we all learned each other's names and majors and introduced 1 new group member). In addition to that, this is the lecture when we started asking a lot of questions and working together quite a bit in our group. The specific focus of this lecture was Codes of Ethics. Some of the questions my group thought about: "How do different professional societies come up with their codes of conduct/ethics?", "What happens when someone violates the code of conduct of a society they're part of?", and "What if you're in 2 different societies with conflicting codes of ethics?". These questions combined with the activity we had during lecture gave me some deeper insight into how different organizations write their codes of conduct and how these codes reflect their values.

    Lecture 3 (2/13/20):

    The two biggest takeaways I had from this lecture were the "seeing with fresh eyes" activity and the listening "ladder" (phases of listening). The "seeing with fresh eyes" activity had us work in partners to prounounce random words in different ways. We were supposed to say the first word that came to mind after hearing the other person's word, and pronouncing it as if we were "chewing", "extending", and "puncing" the word. This activity really had me actively think about how I pronounce words and how other people pronounce words. The listening ladder helped me understand the different levels of listening, and how getting better at all these levels of will help you get better at really comprehending the information that other people are trying to convey. This lecture also had me think about which phase of listening I engaged in the most, and in which situations I engage different levels of listening.

    Lecture 4 (2/18/20):

    This lecture's topics of privacy, information security, and algorithmic bias. Since I am a computer science major, these topics are pretty important and significant in my career. If I end up chosing a career path in software engineering, I may end up writing algorithms that deal with huge amounts of user data. It'll be important for me to have ethical concerns in mind when dealing with these issues when designing applications that deal with a lot of data. Something in particular that I found interesting from the material of this lecture is the idea that algorithms can have bias that wasn't intended by the designers of the programs. Since machine learning algorithms aren't directly written by humans and instead "learn" by processing huge amounts of data, these algorithms can be affected by the quality of the data that they're fed. If the data that these algorithms are given is not truly representative of the population that ends up getting affected by these algorithms, there could be ethical issues that arise as a result of this potential "algorithmic bias". Overall, I found this lecture really interesting as it was intreguing to learn about ethical concerns in computer science.

    Lecture 5 (2/20/20):

    During this lecture, we really emphasized the importance of building design capabilities. We talked about the design capabilities worksheet (referenced in works cited) and how important it is that engineers are well-rounded. I actually really liked this discussion, because personally I really agree with the idea that engineers should work on being well-rounded people. Although most engineers are quite proficient in analytical and mathematical thinking, something that engineers often struggle with other communication and creative skills. The design skills worksheet includes 8 design abilities: "design your design work", "communicate deliberately", "navigate ambiguity", "move between concrete and abstract", "build and craft intentionally", "experiment rapidly", "synthesize information", and "learn from others". If an engineer can manage to improve their abilities in all of these areas, they can really be even more effective in their jobs.

    Lecture 6 (2/25/20):

    Lecture 6 focused on media intelligence: "practices that allow people to access, critically evaluate, and create or manipulate media". This was also the same week when we had the podcast episode assigned in place of one of our regular RR assignments. This was the class's first introductino to the concept of a person's "filter bubble", and how we often isolate ourselves from other people's perspectives in our own filter bubble. This means that we intentionally surround ourselves with information that fits our own opinions. For me, the real takeaway from this lecture was the concept of storytelling, and how important it is. We learned about the spine of a story and how there are specific parts that help build a story:
    1. Once upon a time... (setup)
    2. Every day... (background)
    3. But one day... (plot twist)
    4. Because of that... (result --> times as many times as necessary)
    5. Until, finally... (conclusion)
    This structure of storytelling is something that I've never thought about before, and it gave me a better understanding of how I can make my own stories more effective at conveying information. Overall, this lecture made me feel pretty good about the concepts of listening and storytelling, and also how they connect with ethics in engineering.

    Lecture 7 (2/27/20):

    This lecture was the last lecture that I spent working with my first lecture group, which was definitely a bittersweet moment. The most memorable part of the class was the metaphor activity, where each group was asked to think of a metaphor that represents how their group works as a unit. The metaphor that my group came up with is a "Brick Wall". Some reasons for why our group was a big wall:
    • Each of us is a brick and we build upon one another
    • We're stronger together
    • We get better over time as the bricks come together
    • We BUILD upon each others ideas
    • We're not as strong on our own
    I think the metaphor was definitely a great way to describe our dynamic as a group. Hiteesh, Zihan, Gabe, Kieran and I had some great conversations about ethics engineering over the course of the four weeks that we were together as a group. Like I said at the beggining, it was a bittersweet moment for all of us; we were definitely upset that we wouldn't be working together, but were hopeful for what was in our future.
